# What Survives Platform Failure?

## Core question

Modern organizations increasingly depend on cloud platforms for:

- identity
- communication
- workflow
- records
- coordination
- operational continuity

The critical question is not only:

> Can the platform stay online?

The deeper question is:

> What survives if the platform becomes unavailable, compromised, delayed, or untrusted?

---

## Survival categories

### 1. Identity continuity

Can the organization still verify:

- students
- staff
- administrators
- operators
- trusted escalation paths

outside the platform?

---

### 2. Communication continuity

Can the organization still:

- notify users
- verify important communication
- reconstruct notices
- preserve communication proof

outside the platform?

---

### 3. Operational continuity

Can daily operations continue?

Examples:

- classroom continuity
- emergency workflows
- support operations
- coordination
- approvals
- recovery procedures

---

### 4. Records continuity

Can the organization still access:

- assignments
- records
- submissions
- approvals
- timelines
- audit history

without vendor dependency?

---

### 5. Proof continuity

Can the organization independently verify:

- what happened
- when it happened
- who acted
- what changed
- what was trusted

without relying solely on vendor-provided reporting?

---

## The continuity problem

Many organizations unknowingly allow platforms to become:

- workflow systems
- identity systems
- communication systems
- continuity systems
- proof systems

all at the same time.

This creates concentrated trust dependency.

---

## The trust layer approach

A trust layer helps organizations preserve:

- independent proof
- continuity visibility
- communication verification
- vendor-aware recovery
- identity continuity
- platform-independent auditability

without requiring full platform replacement.

---

## Closing principle

A platform outage is operational.

A trust failure is institutional.

Organizations need infrastructure that survives both.
