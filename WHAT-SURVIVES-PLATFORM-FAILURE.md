# What Survives Platform Failure?

If your most critical platform went down today, what would you still control, prove, access, and trust?

That question is not theoretical.

Platform outages happen. Vendor compromises happen. Account suspensions happen. Data migrations fail. Contracts end. Systems get deprecated. Increasingly, platforms become targets not only because of what they store, but because of how much trust has been concentrated inside them.

Most institutions discover their answer to this question during an incident.

This document helps them find the answer before it matters.

---

## The four survival questions

Every institution dependent on a cloud platform should be able to answer four questions without logging into that platform.

### 1. What do we still control?

Identity records, communication channels, critical files, access lists, and operating procedures that exist and function independently of the vendor.

### 2. What can we still prove?

Evidence of who acted, what changed, what was communicated, what was accessed, and what decisions were made in a form that survives vendor unavailability.

### 3. What can we still access?

The minimum set of records, communications, workflows, and operating materials required to continue essential activity regardless of platform state.

### 4. What can we still trust?

Verified identity and communication channels that have not been compromised, spoofed, locked away, or made unreachable by the failure event.

If any of these answers is unclear, that is the gap.

---

## What typically does not survive

When institutions audit their platform dependencies honestly, the same gaps often surface.

### Identity proof

Login credentials, single sign-on flows, and role validation are often vendor-hosted. If the platform or identity provider is unavailable, so is the confidence layer around who can act.

### Communication history

Emails, messages, announcements, and records stored exclusively inside a platform may not be retrievable during a failure or dispute.

### Access logs

Who accessed what, when, and from where is often only visible through vendor-controlled audit tools.

### Student and staff records

Course completions, grades, documents, submissions, and communications may exist only inside the platform unless exported or mirrored.

### Recovery contacts

Emergency communication lists, escalation paths, and continuity contacts are often stored in the same systems that have failed.

### Proof of prior decisions

Board approvals, policy acknowledgments, signed agreements, and governance records may be trapped inside vendor-managed environments.

## Minimum viable trust layer

This is not a complete security architecture.

It is the minimum a prepared institution should be able to demonstrate.

### Identity continuity

At least one verified, vendor-independent identity record for every staff member, student, administrator, or user whose access or role matters during disruption.

### Communication proof

An independent record of critical communications, not stored exclusively in a vendor-managed environment. This includes emergency contacts, crisis communication chains, and governance notices.

### Access snapshot

A periodic, offline-accessible record of who has access to what, sufficient to reconstruct permissions without requiring the platform to be available.

### Record continuity

Core operational records, including enrollment, completion, credentialing, contracts, or critical approvals, exist in at least one location that does not require the failed platform to retrieve.

### Proof chain

For any decision, action, or communication that carries institutional weight, a record exists that can be verified outside the system where it originated.

## The imitation risk layer

Platform failure is not the only scenario that exposes these gaps.

If an attacker gains access to a platform and uses it to impersonate trusted users, the institution faces a different version of the same problem.

Communications may appear legitimate because they come from familiar channels. Requests may match expected tone, role, timing, and workflow. Approvals may be granted before the compromise is detected.

An independent trust layer does not eliminate this risk.

It creates friction against it by establishing that certain decisions, communications, and identity claims must be verifiable outside the platform before they carry weight.

---

## The audit starting point

Begin with three questions.

1. If this platform were unavailable for 72 hours starting now, what would we lose the ability to do, prove, or access?
2. Which of those losses would be recoverable, and which would be permanent?
3. What percentage of our critical communications, identity records, and access history exists in only one location, and is that location vendor-controlled?

The answers map the dependency.

The dependency maps the exposure.

---

## This is not about leaving your platform

This framework does not recommend replacing platforms.

Most platforms in active institutional use provide genuine value. The goal is not migration. The goal is not redundancy for its own sake.

The goal is removing sole custody from any single vendor.

Keep the platform.

Remove sole custody.

A platform can deliver workflow without owning all trust. Institutions that understand this distinction are better positioned to operate, recover, and prove regardless of what any single vendor does next.
