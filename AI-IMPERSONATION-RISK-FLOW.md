# AI Impersonation Risk Flow

## The shift

Traditional cybersecurity focused heavily on unauthorized access.

AI-enabled fraud expands the threat surface beyond access.

Attackers can increasingly use exposed context, timing, relationships, institutional familiarity, and communication patterns to create believable impersonation attempts.

---

## Example flow

### Step 1 — Exposure

A platform incident exposes:

- names
- emails
- schedules
- institutional relationships
- internal communication context
- course or workflow familiarity

---

### Step 2 — Context reconstruction

Attackers reconstruct:

- who knows who
- who reports to whom
- common communication timing
- trusted formatting patterns
- expected requests
- institutional tone

---

### Step 3 — AI-assisted generation

AI systems help generate:

- realistic phishing attempts
- impersonation emails
- fake notices
- believable escalation requests
- fake continuity instructions
- social engineering sequences

---

### Step 4 — Trust exploitation

The target may trust the interaction because:

- the names are familiar
- the context is believable
- the timing appears normal
- the workflow appears expected
- the platform relationship already exists

---

## The problem

A successful login does not necessarily prove trusted communication.

Institutions increasingly need independent verification around:

- communication authenticity
- workflow escalation
- sensitive requests
- identity continuity
- access-event visibility

---

## The missing layer

Organizations need trust infrastructure capable of helping verify:

- who initiated communication
- whether escalation behavior is abnormal
- whether context matches expected patterns
- whether continuity assumptions remain trustworthy

---

## Closing principle

AI fraud is not only attacking systems.

It is attacking institutional confidence.

One login is not enough.

Trust needs its own layer.
